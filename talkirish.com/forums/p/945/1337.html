    Dia is Muire duit,

    Of course you can ask about Ulster-Scots here! I can't answer all your questions, but I'll try and give you some help.

    I don't know what language your family would have spoken either in Antrim or in Scotland in the 16th century. They could have spoken any of the following:

    • Irish
    • English
    • Scottish Gaelic (ancient Celtic language, like Irish, still surviving mostly in the Scottish highlands)
    • Scots (a mixture of Scots Gaelic and English, still spoken widely in Scotland today)
    • several of the above

    Irish was spoken in Ulster at that time, as were English, Scots and Scottish Gaelic.

    Ulster-Scots is spoken in Northern Ireland, and also in parts of Donegal. To my ear, it sounds like a mix between the Northern Irish accent and the Scottish accent, but uses some words and constructions that are particular to it. Estimated numbers of speakers range from 30,000 to 100,000.

    Where I grew up, we used a lot of words that are described as Ulster-Scots e.g. drooth (thirst), sleekit (sneaky), and of course 'wee' (small), but we would not have considered ourselves Ulster-Scots speakers. Part of this is because the Ulster-Scots movement was considered a Unionist movement and was associated with the Unionist tradition and identity. People from a nationalist tradition would be more likely to identify their way of speaking as being Hiberno-English - that is, being more influenced by the Irish language.

    I am NOT a linguist, and I'm glad, for they all seem to be fighting over what Ulster-Scots is (is it a language? Is it a dialect? Is it an accent?), who speaks it, why, where and how.

    I know you want to learn your ancestor's language. But it will be very difficult for you to first correctly identify and then reconstruct the language they spoke back in the 16th century. The way I see it, a Spanish speaker who decides to learn English to learn more about their English ancestry wouldn't try to learn Shakespearean English  They'd just learn modern English, and use that as a gateway.

    By learning Ulster Irish, you will have a great window into Irish across the island of Ireland, but also into Scottish Gaelic in Scotland, as the languages have similarities.

    If you want to get a better handle on Ulster Scots, check out the links below for audio. Try to listen while reading a transcript.I don't know of any official courses that teach Ulster-Scots, but if you want to learn more, check out this wikipedia article for a good overview of Ulster Scots history and development.

    http://www.ulsterscotsagency.com/

    A good friend of mine worked on creating this website for BBC Northern Ireland - it's got a great collection of Ulster-Scots interviews with audio files.

    Learn more about Scots and Scottish Gaelic here:

    http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scots_language

    PS One last little story. When I was 18, I left Tyrone in Northern Ireland to go to college in Trinity, in Dublin. This was a very 'posh' university, and I was quite naive at the time. People often commented on my accent. I remember one girl (who sounded English to me, but was actually 'posh' Dublin) said to me 'What part of Scotland are you from?'. I'd got used to people thinking I was Scottish at this point, so I just said 'Tyrone' back to her, thinking that she'd realise her mistake and laugh. However, she just said 'Oh really. Is that the highlands or the lowlands?'.

    Seanduine, a chara,

    You're very right that lots of Protestant Scots were given land during the Plantation of Ulster. Many Protestant English were also given land. This was in an attempt colonise and pacify the territory. Read more at wikipedia http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plantation_of_Ulster. Unfortunately, the tensions resulting from the plantations have persisted until today.

    But before the plantation of Ulster, the North of Ireland had very close links to Scotland, to its culture and language. In the days before we had roads, our rivers and seas were our highways. You can see Scotland from much of the North, and close connections have existed between the countries for years.

    I've heard it said that years ago, people in Antrim used to row to Scotland for a dance, that being an easier night out than taking a poor road to somewhere on the island of Ireland! I believe that Irish was spoken until quite recently in the Glens of Antrim - the 1950s? Sorry - none of this is an area I'm particularly expert on.

    Another language that's often missed in comparisons between Scottish Gaelic and Irish is the Manx language (once extinct, now being reintroduced at schools and in learning circles). It is very similar to both Scottish Gaelic and Irish, except that it was written down by an English man, so it's phonetic. It makes it quite easy for an English speaker to pick up a basic words and phrases.
