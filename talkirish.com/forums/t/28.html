    Hi Wee DD,

    If you arrived on our website expecting a site for children, I can understand your disappointment. I created this site to help adults learn Irish. Many of our resources are useful for children and are widely used in schools (there's even a primary school in Saudi Arabia using our site to teach children Irish there!), but the site is not aimed at children in particular. 

    You might have seen that we've just launched our new online Irish course. You'll find it here: http://talkirish.com/online-irish-course It teaches the official standard Irish and is fantastic for both adults and children. We've spend a lot of time and effort to made it really easy to use and fun with georgous, children-friendly graphics to appeal to everybody. One of our user even emailed us a couple of days ago to let us know that her 3 years old has already powered through the first lesson and is well on the way to completing the whole course. Give it a try!

    Another resource we have that children love is our WordFlash games: http://talkirish.com/games - it's a fun way to revise vocabulary while competing with friends. 

    I'm afraid I can't direct you to another site - there aren't really a wealth of Irish language resources out there, which is why we created Talk Irish - perhaps another user can help you out.

    I'm interested to hear your children 'disagree' with the pronounciation on our website. Which particular pronounciations? The free resources on our site are recorded using a native speaker who speaks Ulster Irish. Our online course and MP3 course are based on the gold standard Buntús Cainte, which teaches an caighdean oifigiuil - the official standard Irish - which is different to Ulster Irish.

    I personally think it's important that your children don't learn to 'disagree' with pronounciations of Irish that differ to their own. For the future of the language, it's very important that they learn how to understand pronunciations that are different to theirs. Just as someone like me, from Tyrone can understand the word 'eight' in English, whether it's spoken by an Englishman (ay-it) or a Derry man (ate). If we all only learned to understand English exactly as we speak it, we'd soon be in trouble. The same with Irish.

    Good luck with your search for a site that you find helpful to your children.

    Slán,
    Michelle
