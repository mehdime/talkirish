    Hi, Cathy.

    I'm no expert (on either Irish or Italian), but I'm thinking you could apply the direct conjugation of the verb to this situation rather than using "Beidh" and the verbal noun:

    Tiocfaidh bás agus beidh do shúile aige.

    This maintains the full parallel with the original.  Beidh is still correct for the second part of the phrase because it's needed for the idiom "to have".  The only other possible convolution of this that I can think of is whether the Italian poet is addressing a broader audience than one -- is "tuoi" the plural "your" or is it signular?

    A quick check online says that "tuoi" is the plural of "tuo" "your", so to be completely correct in Irish, we need to change to a plural possessive second person:

    Tiocfaidh bás agus beidh bhur shúile aige.

    Death will come and will have your (plural) eyes.  This changes the scope of the statement to apply universally, rather than addressing an individual, so I think it's an important distinction.

    If there are any other refinements needed, I hope someone more experienced and fluent will chime in....

    Hi folks, I don't speak any Italian but I think Dale has been misled by online translation software. Tuoi is a plural form, but the fact is that plurality in words like this in languages like French (and I think Italian) refers to the thing(s) owned and not to the owner and I suspect that this is the case here - that the tu is singular but that the tuoi form is used because the things owned are multiple. Hope that makes sense - it's a bit complicated. Anyway, I think that it should be do here rather than bhur.

    The sentence as a whole is ambiguous in English and also in Irish if you translate it as "Tiocfaidh an bás agus beidh do shúile aige." Does it mean that death will come and pluck the eyes out of your head, or does it mean that death will come and will see the world as you do or resemble you in some way, or is the poet talking to a lover and saying that when death comes the eyes of death will resemble the lover's eyes? I don't know. I suppose it's poetry and ambiguity goes with the territory!

    Haigh, trinantron.

    I'm sure seánó will answer in due course, but your query is interesting on several levels.

    Irish language constructs on the subject of love are quite different from English, and I fear the construct you have asked about is an English one, not an Irish one.

    The basic format for telling someone you love them is to say, "I have love in my heart for you" -- Tá grá agam duitse.  This uses the Irish idiom "Love is at me" which is translated "I have love".  The final word, duitse, is the emphatic form of the prepositional pronoun duit "to you".  the Irish word for "heart" croí does not appear in the sentence at all, but is implied through the emphatic suffix on duit.

    The expression "my love for you" is not an independent phrase in English, so an attempt to translate it into Irish may depend very much on how it is applied in a complete sentence.  You could say "mo ghrá duit" but I haven't seen such a construct in my reading.  I think you may be hard-pressed to get a satisfactory response to this query without some context.  Perhaps Is duit mo ghrá could work, but that might be translated "You are my love" or "My love is for you".  A similar idea, but not exactly definitive....

    Irish seems to prefer to express "love" as a noun rather than a verb.  The phrase "I love you" would be unusual in Irish because "love" seldom appears as a verb.  I have found a couple of instances of it, in biblical passages, Romans 13:9 being one, and Matthew 22:37, 39 also, each for a similar use.  For me, that simply says that trying to employ the Irish word grá "love" in similar ways as we employ "love" in English could lead to some issues.  Your example may or may not be one of them, but my sense is that this would not be a typical construct as Gaeilge.

    Slán!

    Sorry Dale, I think I threw you a bit of a curved ball there.

    Ba chóir dom means I should have. Leagan cainte literaly means a laying out of speech, so it's a turn of phrase, Níos simplí is simpler. And a roghnú is just to choose.

    So the whole thing together is I should have a simpler turn of phrase to choose, or to put it another way, I should have chosen a simpler turn of phrase. That structure is confusing because it isn't like the English in terms of word order.
