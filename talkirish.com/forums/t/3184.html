    Hi, Interesting question. Apparently Dia dhuit tráthnóna does exist - I've just Googled it and it is endorsed by sources I would consider trustworthy enough but as an Ulster speaker I've never heard it and I certainly wouldn't use it. To me it sounds like something which has been produced under the influence of English but it would be foolish to condemn it as wrong if it's used by native Irish speakers in some dialects.

    BrídMhór is right in saying that Dia d(h)uit can be used at any time and there is no need to specify the time. It is also worth pointing out that tráthnóna in Irish means both afternoon and evening, so this isn't really a strict equivalent to Good afternoon anyway.

    As for Tráthnóna maith, this seems to me like Lá breá for Good day. It is very definitely Béarlachas (English-influenced Irish). Compare this to French, where people don't greet each other with bon matin or bon après-midi, and this doesn't seem to hamper normal communication or make French Academicians give up and speak English in despair at the limitations of their native tongue. After all, even if you've had a couple of glasses of vin ordinaire with (or indeed for) breakfast you should still have a rough idea what time of day it is without someone having to remind you. (Apparently, the English DO need reminding... Strange!)

    Bonjour Sean! 

    En Français, you could say "bon matin", & there's nothing technically wrong with it, but it is a very unusual usage; people just don't say it. En Français, we say "Bonjour" [Good Day] or "Ciao" [Hi there], although in the evening, French people do say "Bon soir", lit., "Good Evening". While people do say "Bonne Nuit", usually, they only say that when they're saying goodbye to people in the evening. "Bonne Nuit" carries the meaning of "Good Night & Good-Bye". In certain situations in the evening, "Bonne Nuit" can be substituted for "Au Revoir" [lit., "until we see each other again"], but usually, when French-speaking people say "good-bye" to each other, they say "Au Revoir", or "Ciao". The latter is extremely informal, and therefore not used very often in French, which is a very formal and polite language.

    Bonjour et merci beaucoup! I am glad of that confirmation, as my French is not great and I was keeping my fingers crossed that I was correct! Apparently you can say bon après-midi as well but not as a greeting, only when you are saying goodbye and wishing someone a pleasant afternoon. Apologies to anyone who thinks a discussion of French is inappropriate here but it's just to back up the point that you can't necessarily find exact equivalents for words between languages, and this doesn't mean that the other languages are somehow inferior or less expressive because they don't parcel up reality in the same packages.

     And while we're on the subject of French, I should perhaps point out the massive debt that Irish owes to French. Common words like garsún/gasúr (garcon), páiste (page) or seomra (chambre) are derived from French or Norman-French. Other words which many people assume are derived from English are also French or Norman - muisiriún is derived from Old French moucheron, (in turn derived from mouche meaning fly); damhsa is also from Norman French.

    And how did we repay you? By called rats francaigh and syphilis "an bholgach fhrancach" (the French pox!) Quels ingrats, ces irlandais!

    Actually, I think you've gotten that just backwards- I think it's French that owes a huge debt to Gaelic! Gaelic is a much, much, older language than French. Looking at Old French, it wasn't a well-defined language that was clearly distinguished from the other forms of debased Latin used in Medieval Europe until the 12th century C.E. The embroidered language on the Bayuex tapestry isn't Old French, it's Medieval Latin. Since I grew up speaking French, I was always interested in all the ways that French isn't like Latin, & I alsways wondered where all that other stuff came from- until I started to study Gaelic instead of just hearing a few words from my grandmothers. Once I started studying Gaelic, everything fell into place. French is what you get when you cross Latin & Ancient Gaulish, a Celtic language. Granted, Gaulish was a Brythonic language, not a Goidelic one, like Gaelic, but since it was so many thousands of years ago, the Brythonic & Goidelic languages were both much closer to their common ancestral Celtic language, from before we had the two branches that encompass the six modern Celtic lanaguages, which are Irish, Scots, & Manx Gaelic on the Goidelic saide, and Welsh, Cornish & Breton on the Brythonic side, in case anyone's interested. It also must be mentioned that Seano is right about the French currently spoken as being Norman-derived. The Official French, as outlined by the Academie Française, which is in charge of enforcing the Official standards & protecting "la langue sacrée", "the sacred tongue", is the Langue d'Oeil or Northern French, as used & developped by the Normans. However, despite what the Academie Française would like to have people believe, all French isn't Northern, and it took a long time for them to round up all the various dialects of French, set an official standard, & get everyone to go along with it. Because of the power & influence wielded by the Angevin Empire (in the North, the Angevin family is from Anjou, thus the name) the Official standard favors the Northern dialects over the Langue D'Oc, or southern region, including the Kingdom of L'Occitan. My point, and I do have one, is that French is a Medieval language, and didn't exist before then, whereas Gaelic is one of the world's oldest languages, being of a similar age to Sanskrit, so I think it is really French that owes a huge debt to Gaelic, so let me just say "Merci Beaucoup aux L'Irlandais! Je vous remerci mille fois!" "Thank you very much to the Irish people! I thank you a thousand times [polite form]!"
