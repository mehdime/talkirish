    The Little Prince Quote

    Reply | Contact

    Hello, everyone.

    I have been reading "An Prionsa Beag", or "The Little Prince" by St. Exupery, reading the Irish and following along with my English copy.  And I have a bit of a conundrum, because, in one of the most famous passages of the work, there is a word used in the Irish translation that I cannot find anywhere.  It is apparently a verbal construct, but...well, I'll give the English version first (which, as I understand it, fairly closely follows the original French), then the Irish translation:

    English (translated from French):  "It is only with the heart that one can see rightly.  What is essential is invisible to the eye."

    Irish (not sure if translated from French or English)  "Is leis an chroí is fearr a tímid.  Na rudaí is bunúsaí amuigh, ní feidir leis na súile iad a fheiceáil."

    The word I cannot find in the online dictionary, at least, is "tímid", which I assume is part of a verb conjugation because of the "-mid" ending, but I am not finding anything with the root part, nor do I find anything under any word listed under the definition of "see".  My translation of the first line of the quote would be, "It is with the heart that we see best."  But that assumes that "tímid" means "we see" perhaps in the imperative mood, but I don't find that form of word under "feach" or "feic".  There were other words listed for "see", but none of them were verbs, or at least not independent verbs.

    So can someone please help me with the derivation of "tímid"?

    Another interesting part of the quote for me is the use of "bunúsaí amuigh" for "essential.  "Bunúsaí" seems to be the plural of "bunús" which means "most" or "basis", and seems to descend from "bunú" which means foundation.  So I get the idea of "essential" being "foundational," but I was intrigued by the addition of "amuigh" or "out" to the idiom.  Looking at some of the idioms using "amuigh" I would look at this as meaning "foundationally obvious" or "foundationally apparent", which certainly conveys the idea of "essential."  But my dictionaries give "bunúsach" as meaning "essential"; so why not use that single word instead of this other construct?  Was this simply translator's choice, or is there a good reason that I'm missing?  Obviously "bunúsach" is closely related to "bunúsaí"; could it be a plural form of "bunúsach"  If so, why is "amuigh" needed?  Just trying to understand the idiom in its entirety....

    Thanks for any feedback!

    Dale D

    Haigh folks. I'm back again! Críostóir is right about this. There is a word feiceann which i used in Irish a lot but many speakers also use the word chíonn (in Southern dialects) and tíonn (in Northern dialects). The first one is pronounced like a breathy h - hee-an, while tíonn is pronounced chee-an. At some point, someone decided to put the two together and allow people to pronounce the word however they want, as tchíonn. So you will sometimes see forms like tchífidh mé (I'll see). Personally, I think this looks pretty ugly and I can't see any reason not to write tíonn/chíonn as you say it. What do you think of the book? I've never read it but it's on my list! Beirigí bua, Seáno.

    Hi Dale, No problem. Yes, as you say, tímid is the northern version of chímid. I was away in France for a couple of weeks, which was great! The question about Tí Dia or Chí Dia is interesting. Many non-native speakers would make sin and sinn the same but in so far as I would use sinn, it would be a much stronger ny sound at the end, or perhaps a ng, as you say. It is difficult because sinn doesn't really exist in the north - we tend to say muid in all circumstances. Linn is definitely a liny or ling sound, an n with the tongue lying against the top of the mouth. A double n very often changes the quality of the vowel - ann is pronounced more like awn than an. That's my perception anyway. Maybe other people have light to shed on this question?

    Reading children's books is a good way of learning a language. Like I say, I'll get hold of a copy of An Prionsa Beag. There is another good children's book, Emil agus na Bleachtairí (Emil und die Detektive) which is very popular in Germany. It was translated by Nicholas Williams with the original illuistrations. You can order Irish language books from Litríocht.com. online. If you have any questions on your further reading, let me know!
