    An interesting fact (well, I think it's interesting, anyway!) The Irish word for prayer is paidir, which is an Irish version of Pater as in Pater Noster, Our Father in Latin. In other words, this is THE prayer in Irish. There is a really nice rhyming version of it in Irish which someone composed a couple of hundred years ago. It's in a book called Croí Cine, which unfortunately I lent to someone a while back, so if anyone out there is "in áit na garaíochta" (in a position to oblige) and has a copy of the book, could you have a look through it and post the rhyming Paidir here! If no-one finds it, I'll hunt down a copy myself and post it here in the New Year. Slán!

    The problem is that inaccuracies can become viral very quickly on the net and spread like wildfire. It is amazing how many websites offer "Breithlá Shona duit" or "Lá Breithe Shona duit" as the correct Irish for Happy Birthday to you, when the correct Irish is Breithlá Sona or Lá Breithe Sona duit. There is even a site selling tee-shirts and cards with the incorrect version. On the other hand, a non-Irish girl who had the temerity to put an item on Youtube about how to say Happy Birthday in Irish and happened to use the wrong version was flamed (i.e. insulted on line) by lots of irate Gaels rather than being applauded for trying to help. Insisting on correctness is one thing, being rude to people is another!  

    People tend to trust things they see on the web, just as they used to trust books, and once a mistake has spread and gone viral it is very hard to shift it. Many generations ago, there was a Japanese text book for learners of English which included the word wears in the sense of clothes. The author must have assumed that since you can say Menswear and Outdoor Wear you can also use wears on its own for any kind of clothing, and frequent repetition and a lack of exposure to native English have compounded the problem. In my experience, it is almost impossible to persuade Japanese speakers of English that sentences like "You are wearing your best wears today!" is completely and abjectly wrong. My wife was once warned by a Japanese teacher to avoid the cycle track outside the village because "a man with no wears has been seen there", which inspires wonderful images of an itinerant ironmonger wandering through the rice paddies looking for his lost bundle of DIY utensils.
