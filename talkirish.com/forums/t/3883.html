    The page on wiktionary is really interesting. Most of them are good. Words like slew from slua, or phoney from fáinne, or smithereens from smidiríní are well-known. There is one there which is doubtful, the idea that kybosh comes from caidhp bháis. The problem is that there is a complete lack of evidence that the phrase "an chaidhp bháis" was ever used in Irish in contexts like this, though it is recorded as a word for a death-cap mushroom. 

    I think the idea about yeah is interesting. Ultimately, I don't think that yeah comes from Irish, because English is closely related to languages like Dutch and German, both of which use ja for yes, but the idea that people might have selected yeah rather than yes in Ireland because of its similarity to the Irish sea is certainly reasonable. Unfortunately, it would be impossible to prove it conclusively either way.

    Re: Irish words in English

    Reply | Contact

    Hi Chelle, Aye is used quite a lot in the North of England and in Lowland Scots. It probably exists in Scots Gaelic as well (as a loan-word), and it is found quite commonly in Ulster Irish. People often say things like "Aidhe, bhal!" (aye, well) in Ulster Irish. I think it must come from northern varieties of English. There are several theories about where aye comes from: either from aye which is the Scots dialect word for "ever" or "always" (which doesn't seem that likely) or from yai/yeah, older dialect forms of yes. So it is basically an English dialect word which has become common in Irish, like craic. There are lots of English words in Irish, some of which are so old that they still preserve the way English was spoken in the time of Chaucer. Good examples are siopa or cóta, which sound like shoppe or coate, the way Chaucer would have written and pronounced the words shop and coat. There are also some interesting survivals like the vocabulary of card-playing. The Irish words for the suits are spéireata (or spád in some dialects), muileata, hárt and triuf. These are derived from Middle English terms - spearheade (spear-head), mullet (originally a term for a star, here used for diamonds, hárt (hearts) and triuf which is related to trefoil (clover). In other words, Irish is a fascinating source of living history for the English language too!

    Hi Malachi, Yes, galore is a good one! It's not only found in the States - it is found all over the English-speaking world. As it corresponds completely in sound and use to the Irish (and Scots Gaelic) go leor, it can be regarded as a definite.

    Unfortunately, scam is one of the Cassidy words I mentioned at the start of this thread. Cassidy ransacked Irish dictionaries looking for chance correspondences of sound between Irish words and American slang terms. He claimed that the phrase "is cam é" (it's crooked) could have been the origin of scam, but this is highly unlikely. "Is cam é" as a phrase is a little odd - "Tá sé cam" would be more usual - and there are almost no examples of phrases in one language becoming nouns in another. The word scam probably derives from scamp, nineteenth century slang for a rogue, which itself derives from a Dutch word for a wanderer.

    There are three candidates for the origin of "so long". Firstly, the Hebrew "shalom". Secondly, various Scandinavian phrases like "farvel sa lange" (farwell for a while). Thirdly, the Irish slán. Of these three, I would be inclined to go for the Scandinavian explanation, as it sounds most like the English. (Slán and shalom aren't very similar to "so long".) And of course, there were a lot of Scandinavian communities in parts of the States in the 19th century. 

    This is exactly why I started this thread - to sort out the wheat from the chaff. Here we have three possibilities, one of which is absolutely correct, another completely wrong and a third possible but not perhaps the best candidate. 

    Any other words which might be of Irish origin?

    I've heard this one before and it's certainly interesting. On the face of it, it looks like a good one. People do say Is maith sin and it sounds like Smashing. But there is a problem. The problem is that there are lots of metaphors for wonderful or great that involve hitting, breaking or smashing. A cracker, a belter, a hit. We would need to know how long smashing has been used in this way and if smash or any other derivative words have been used in the same way. Because if smash predates smashing, that's strong circumstantial evidence that the correspondence between smashing and Is maith sin is just a coincidence. I don't know enough about the history of the terms in English to give a definite answer, so I would classify it as possible but not proven.

    Hi Dale, Yes, well spotted! This is almost certainly the origin of gob. In Irish, you would say "Druid do ghob" or "Dún do ghob" in exactly the same circumstances as you would say "Shut your gob!" in English. There is no convincing origin for it in English, but in Irish it has always meant beak or mouth, and is used in an ancient Irish poem about the blackbird of Loch Lao (Belfast Lough). Another word which is almost certainly of Irish descent is "puss" as in sourpuss or "a smack in the puss". The original Irish word pus is used in exactly the same way and there are a few derivative words, my favourite of which is "pusachán", meaning a pouting, surly person.
